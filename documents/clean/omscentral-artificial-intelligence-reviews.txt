May 4, 2026 spring 2026 I have a feeling this course is going to get review-bombed by some unhappy campers, so I'm here to offer my discourse. To help contextualize my review: I'm a CompEng undergrad, full-time developer for ~4 years now. I am expecting a comfortable A in this class. I previously took AI4R. I've got lots to say about this course, but we'll get this one out of the way first: NOSI. I was worried when I started the course this semester and we were told we would be guinea pigs for NOSI. I would say that most of what has been said about this (funnily enough, kinda how I feel about the reputation of the exams in the course) is very overblown. My experience with NOSI was that it mostly did not get in the way of things. It did choke on Jupyter notebooks, and I would say that course staff should focus on getting that seamless, because it was a bit annoying for P5, causing me to restart Nosi a few times to get my output to show. But this cost me maybe 5 minutes of total time. I do use VSCode at work, so I'm used to the interface, and I run a pretty normie setup on a Mac M1. If you run some custom Linux kernel and use, I don't know, emacs for everything, you will probably not find Nosi as seamless as I did. The TA who rolled out Nosi was also extremely helpful. Yuhui deserves a named shoutout, because he did an incredibly unpopular task very well, and I saw probably hundreds of posts from him through the semester as he helped debug issues across all the different variants of Nosi. He gets paid probably way too little for the job he's done this semester. With that out of the way, let's get onto the real review. I really mulled over taking this course, as the reviews make it seem a lot worse and a lot more dysfunctional than it is. I'd like to dispel some rumors, as I really believe this is a must-do course for students who actually want to pursue ML/AI: Myths: "This course teaches outdated and irrelevant techniques." This is categorically not true. It is true that you will not learn state-of-the-art methods to build a VLA or LLM. Instead, this course can be seen as two things: (1) a broad survey of the general field of artificial intelligence in all forms, making it a "map" to other courses in the field (ML, DL, RL) (2) a foundational course that teaches you patterns you will see in these other courses. This is a course about making intractable problems tractable (and specifically, implementing algorithms to do so). There are several themes that you see throughout the course that carry over to modern machine learning: Bayes Nets introduce the idea of representing probability through structured graphs; search introduces the idea of visualizing problems as moving through a landscape, finding heuristics that effectively move through this space, and overcoming local minimum (simulated annealing, SGD, momentum); introducing hidden states to find structure in data (EM, HMM). All of these are foundational ideas to the rest of the field, and make this a great first class for the specialization. "This course is disorganized, especially the exams." I almost did not take this course because of the discourse on the exams. However, I had a positive experience with both. The midterm in particular, was a great chance to solidify concepts and work through problems by hand, and I found most of it stuck with me as I came across it on the final. It's a great way to actually learn something that sticks. The final was a bit more broad, and covered some material that required more digging through the book to learn on the fly, but nothing was unfair. The final had 2 true "corrections", 8 "warnings", and 7 "further info" clarifications: all color-coded, broken out by section. For a 28 page final with ~50 questions in a class of ~1000 students, it seems pretty reasonable to me. Exams were challenging, and I spent ~25 hours on the midterm over the week and ~15 on the final. If you try to do it all in a weekend, it will feel overwhelming, but doing ~1 section a day is a really nice pace. It's also worth remembering that the midterm + final are collectively 35% of your grade, so doing well on the projects means you can avoid a lot of the stress here. With a 93 on the midterm and a 99 on the projects, I only needed a 34 on the final to keep an A. One final comment on the "dysfunctional" myth: there is an army of TAs to support in this course, and they do a great job of helping out, hosting office hours, etc. There is a lot going on, but the TAs do a really good job of making it clear what you do and don't need to focus on. "This course is unreasonably difficult." This course is a very achievable A. If you are a well-versed programmer with an engineering undergrad, you shouldn't find anything too difficult to solve. In terms of difficulty "peaks", I don't have much of anything other reviewers don't already cover: Pr1 and Pr2 are the most difficult programming assignments, but Pr5 is the hardest conceptually (and most rewarding, imo). You do need to grasp probability, but the "linear algebra" of this course maxes out at understanding the multivariate Gaussian. The course is structured so that you can still make a very comfortable A without taking the most demanding points: I got a 99 on PR1 without trying the tri-A*, and a 95 on Pr2 because I opted not to beat Peter's agent. The hardest part of the course, like most, is time management. The common advice here is that the course is frontloaded and it's easy once you get past PR1/2. However, the busiest portion of the course to me was the middle, as you have a lot going on between Assignment 3, multiple challenge questions being due, the midterm, and immediately launching into Assignment 4. I started feeling like I could breathe again after Pr5, and the last month was pretty chill. All in all, this is a 5/5 OMSCS course and a 4-4.5/5 AI course. My biggest points against it would be that there are a few lectures that feel half-baked (MDP / POMDP in particular is way too short and thrown in at the end). I think that there could've been more focus on the idea of the "how do we represent problems in an intractable domain" aspect of the course (most of my understanding here came from the book). I also kind of wish that the course kept the First-Order Logic lectures - I actually enjoyed the Facebook lectures on DL, but you don't really do anything meaningful with the DL covered in the class, and FOL might've better tied together some of the other themes of the class. Rating: 5 / 5 Difficulty: 4 / 5 Workload: 12 hours / week

January 4, 2026 fall 2025 This is an exceptionally difficult class. For context: I likely have more experience with Python than the average OMSCS student, but less overall CS experience. I took several related classes to prepare me for this one (KBAI, Game AI, AI4R, etc.), and it was still rough. But how difficult you find each assignment greatly depends on your familiarity with each section, and what your natural aptitudes are. Additionally, part of the difficulty comes from the strict plagiarism policy, which limits the external resources you can consult. Here's a breakdown of each assignment: A1 - A*. ~60 hours if you haven't taken a graduate-level class that teaches A* at a high rigor, ~20-30 with. Expect extra time if you want a 90%+. It took me ~40, and I found it to be the easiest assignment both to conceptualize and to program, despite most students saying that it's by far the hardest. The time investment comes from implementation size rather than conceptual difficulty. A2 - Game playing. ~40h if you're not comfortable with recursion and debugging complex recursive algorithms, ~20h with. I had a terrible time with this assignment due to misinterpreting the provided documentation, but otherwise thought it was conceptually straightforward. A3 - Bayes nets. If your understanding of graduate-level stats is solid, this assignment can be done in 5-10 hours. Otherwise, you're in for a rough couple weeks. I spent ~30 hours prepping for the assignment, and another ~30 on the assignment itself. This assignment requires a tenth of the coding of assignment 1, but it was far more difficult for me. A4 - Machine learning. If you're comfortable with numpy and vectorization, ~20 hours, ~40 without. This was the first assignment I felt that the provided material was not enough to understand the concepts, so I had to do a lot of self study. A5 - Gaussian mixture models. This is a very polarizing assignment. The first half requires what I thought was  obtuse numpy broadcasting and spatial reasoning, and it was easily the worst experience I’ve had in any CS class due to a complete mismatch with my aptitudes. The second half is comparatively trivial and doable in an hour or two. If you have strong linear algebra skills and a good working spatial memory, this will likely be a breeze. Otherwise, expect ~50 hours on the first half alone. A6 - Hidden Markov models. I didn't do this assignment, but the folks that did said it was on par with the difficulty of assignment #4. Difficulty: A5 > A3 >> A4 > A2 > A1 Time taken: A5 > A1 > A3 > A2 > A4 The midterms and exams are a great way to review and solidify your understanding of the material. However, you aren't really graded on your understanding of the material, but rather on how well you can solve dozens of problems without any mechanical errors. Being off by one decimal after 2 pages of math is worth 0 points, as is taking the right approach but making a minor mistake along the way. In addition, there are several ambiguously-worded questions and later-corrected solutions, and I found it to be a stressful experience. There's a 24 hour challenge period after the exam ends where you can argue your case for why your incorrect answer should be marked as correct. Expect your grade to jump as much as 1-2 letter grades (yes, 10-20%) after regrading. I asked for clarifications on a couple questions but was denied due to exam policies, so I had to guess between two answers, and later learned I chose the wrong ones. But overall, I found the TAs to be pretty generous and forgiving with points. Effort expended on exams does not necessarily correlate with a higher grade.
